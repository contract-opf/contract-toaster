# Data handling, classification, retention, and legal hold

Architecture lives in [ARCHITECTURE.md](../ARCHITECTURE.md).

This is the authoritative home for how `contract-toaster` classifies the data it touches, how long it keeps each kind, and how legal hold overrides ordinary purge. [ARCHITECTURE.md](../ARCHITECTURE.md) (Storage) describes the buckets, tables, and purge worker; the operating procedures live in [RUNBOOK.md](../RUNBOOK.md) (document retention, legal hold). Threat enumeration for the data paths described here lives in [docs/threat-model.md](threat-model.md). Where those documents and this one disagree, **this document is canonical for classification, retention, and hold semantics** and the others should be read as linking here.

The governing principle is that this tool processes **legal documents**, and the cost of getting deletion wrong is asymmetric: keeping a document a little too long is a cost-and-privacy problem we can bound, but deleting a document that was under legal hold, or mid-review, is irreversible and is an evidence-destruction risk. Retention is therefore designed to **fail safe toward keeping** in exactly the cases where keeping matters, and to delete only when it is provably safe.

## Document content classification

The original brief treated EIAAs as **low-sensitivity**, and that assumption is doing too much work. An EIAA (Event/Independent Activity Agreement and the related facility-use and program agreements Exos signs) is *usually* low-sensitivity, but the body text is not guaranteed to be. In practice these agreements routinely carry:

- **Facility and premises terms** — site addresses, access provisions, security and incident-reporting obligations for a physical location.
- **Insurance and indemnity terms** — coverage limits, additional-insured language, certificates naming carriers and policy numbers.
- **Compliance and regulatory terms** — background-check, licensing, safety-certification, and audit-cooperation provisions.
- **Student-program terms** — agreements touching minors or student participants, which can carry consent, supervision, and privacy obligations.
- **Healthcare-adjacent terms** — trainer/athletic, medical-clearance, injury-handling, and occasionally PHI-adjacent language.

Any one of these can appear in a document the uploader believed was routine. **We therefore do not classify documents by agreement type; we classify by the controls the worst plausible content requires.** v1 stores and processes everything at the **Confidential** tier (below), and we build the next tier's controls now rather than discovering we need them when the first student-program or healthcare-term agreement lands.

| Tier | Definition | Examples | Controls in v1 |
|------|------------|----------|----------------|
| **Internal** | Non-document system data with no counterparty substance. | Cost ledger figures, token counts, model IDs, version hashes, request timestamps. | KMS at rest, TLS in transit, owner-or-admin read. |
| **Confidential** *(v1 default for all uploaded/generated documents)* | Counterparty agreement text and anything derived from it. Assumed to contain facility, insurance, compliance, student-program, or healthcare-adjacent terms unless proven otherwise. | Uploaded `.docx`, generated redline `.docx`, extracted clause text, retrieved precedent text, model rationale, model summaries. | KMS at rest, TLS in transit, **owner-or-admin** row-level access (see [ARCHITECTURE.md](../ARCHITECTURE.md) Security posture), no document substance in CloudWatch logs (see [docs/threat-model.md](threat-model.md)), retention + legal hold per this doc. |
| **Restricted** *(next tier — controls scaffolded now, not yet exercised)* | Documents a reviewer or admin identifies as carrying regulated content (minors/student PII, PHI-adjacent, named-individual sensitive terms). | A student-program agreement naming minors; an agreement with medical-clearance PII. | Everything in Confidential **plus** named-access lists (not just owner-or-admin), tighter key separation, and mandatory legal-hold eligibility. The `access_scope` field on every `reviews` row exists today so a document can be narrowed to a named set without re-architecting access control. |

**Why scaffold Restricted now.** The row-level-security shape (`owner_sub` + `access_scope` on every review), per-purpose KMS keys, and the legal-hold flags described below are built in v1 specifically so the Restricted tier is a configuration change, not a redesign. We pay that small cost up front because retrofitting access control and hold semantics after sensitive data exists is far more expensive and error-prone. This mirrors the row-level-security rationale in [ARCHITECTURE.md](../ARCHITECTURE.md) (Security posture) and [docs/design-notes.md](design-notes.md).

## Derived corpus artifacts classification

The corpus pipeline produces **derived artifacts** from confidential executed agreements. These artifacts carry the same confidentiality as the source documents and must be classified and lifecycle-governed accordingly. Three stores are in scope:

| Store | Content | Tier | KMS key domain | Lifecycle and hold |
|-------|---------|------|----------------|--------------------|
| **S3 Vectors knowledge-base store** | Embeddings derived from corpus clause text; per-vector filter metadata (clause_id, topic, polarity, snapshot version, playbook_id). Full clause text is **not** stored inline — metadata only. | **Confidential** | **Corpus key domain** (the `corpus` CMK, per [ARCHITECTURE.md](../ARCHITECTURE.md) Storage and reconciliation note from the 2026-06-11 architecture review, issue #32). The corpus key covers S3 Vectors and the clause-text store. | Lifecycle is bound to corpus snapshot manifests: vectors for a retired snapshot are eligible for deletion only after the rollback window expires (see [ARCHITECTURE.md](../ARCHITECTURE.md) Corpus store lifecycle). If a corpus source document is deleted, its derived vectors and clause-text entries are removed from retired snapshots during the next corpus lifecycle sweep; they are exempt from removal while any active or in-window snapshot references them. **Corpus legal hold extends to derived artifacts**: a hold on a corpus source document prevents deletion of its derived embeddings and clause-text entries. |
| **Staging index** (draft snapshot) | A separate S3 Vectors store holding in-progress ingestion for a candidate snapshot, not yet active. Content mirrors the source corpus for that snapshot. | **Confidential** | **Corpus key domain** (same CMK as the active store). | Decommissioned staging indexes — candidate snapshots that were never activated, or staging indexes superseded by a subsequent ingestion run — are destroyed after a configurable grace period (default: 7 days after the candidate's last activity, or immediately on explicit admin discard). A staging index that has been promoted to active is retained as part of the rollback window, not destroyed. |
| **Clause-text store** (S3/DynamoDB, `clause_id`-keyed) | Full clause text fetched by `clause_id` after vector retrieval. Keyed by immutable clause ID; content is counterparty-derived legal text. | **Confidential** | **Corpus key domain** (corpus CMK, per reconciliation note #32). | Clause-text entries are lifecycle-bound to the corpus snapshot that introduced them. An entry becomes eligible for deletion when no active or in-rollback-window snapshot references its `clause_id`. Corpus legal hold prevents deletion of any clause-text entry referenced by a held source document, regardless of snapshot window. |

**Hold semantics for derived artifacts.** A corpus legal hold (see [Legal hold → Hold flags](#hold-flags)) placed on a corpus source document extends unconditionally to all derived artifacts produced from that document: its embeddings in every snapshot store, its staging-index vectors, and its clause-text entries. The hold prevents those entries from being removed by any corpus lifecycle action — snapshot retirement, staging-index decommission, or source-document deletion sweep — until the hold is released. Release restores normal lifecycle eligibility on the next sweep; it does not immediately destroy the artifacts.

**Classification rationale.** These artifacts are derived from executed agreements — confidential counterparty text — and therefore inherit the Confidential tier. Embeddings are not reversible in the cryptographic sense, but they are computed over counterparty-derived clause text, can be used to reconstruct approximate clause content, and must be protected under the same controls as the source. Staging-index content is a superset of the source documents for that draft snapshot. Clause-text entries are direct reproductions of counterparty text. None of these are Internal; all are Confidential.

That is correct **only for metadata that carries no document substance.** "Metadata retained indefinitely" must not become a backdoor that retains the very clause text, rationale, and summaries the retention window was supposed to purge.

This table is the **canonical `reviews` field dictionary** for the whole project: every `reviews` field is named, typed by whether it carries document substance, classified, and given a retention rule **here**, and [ARCHITECTURE.md](../ARCHITECTURE.md) (Storage) and [docs/phase-0-issues.md](phase-0-issues.md) (DynamoDB tables) reference it rather than re-listing fields with divergent names. Fields that carry document substance are **not** in the indefinite-retention set unless the retention policy explicitly names them; they expire with the document.

**Field-name note (canonical):** the snapshotted retention window is `retention_window_at_creation` (a day count); the model identifiers are `primary_model_id` / `critic_model_id` / `embedding_model_id` (not a single `model`); the playbook identifier is `playbook_id` (not `playbook` or `playbook_name`). Use these names everywhere.

**Rule.** The indefinite-retention set is the **non-substantive** columns above: identifiers, hashes, the decision, and the cost/version facts that prove a review happened and on what basis. Anything that reproduces or summarizes the counterparty's text (`verdict_summary`, `issue_rationale_text`) is Confidential and is purged on the **same window as the document it describes** — it does not get a free pass to live forever just because it sits in a table called metadata. Hashes are retained because they prove integrity without reproducing content.

The practical consequence: purging a document (below) deletes the `uploads`/`outputs` objects **and** clears the Confidential substance fields on the matching `reviews` row, while leaving the audit-bearing record (the review happened, this decision, this cost, these hashes, this playbook version) intact.

**Accepted limitation — DynamoDB PITR tail.** The `reviews` table has point-in-time recovery (PITR) enabled (35-day continuous backup). Clearing a Confidential substance field (`verdict_summary`, `issue_rationale_text`, `attorney_disposition_note`) removes it from the live item but **not** from the PITR window: a restore within 35 days could recover the pre-clear value. The `analysis_report` S3 object is **unversioned** (same as `uploads`/`outputs`), so its deletion is immediate and real — the PITR caveat applies only to the DynamoDB row fields, not to the S3-stored report. So **35 days is the true minimum retention floor for substance fields held in DynamoDB**, even under a `0`-day retention setting or an immediate retroactive purge. This is a deliberate, accepted trade-off (PITR is our operational-recovery backstop for the whole table, and substance-in-Dynamo is bounded and access-controlled): the `uploads`/`outputs` S3 objects are **unversioned**, so their deletes are immediate and real, and that is where the bulk of the document substance lives. Reviewers and Legal should treat "purged" as "purged from live storage now, and from PITR within 35 days." If a future requirement needs sub-35-day substance destruction, the remedy is to move substance fields out of the PITR-backed table into unversioned S3; v1 does not.

The same rule applies to immutable audit. Audit rows and the object-locked audit archive may retain **non-substantive proof** indefinitely: event IDs, actor, action, target, decision, topic IDs, hashes, model/playbook/prompt/standard-form/model-policy/corpus identifiers, token counts, cost, scanner rule IDs, authorization outcomes, and **retrieved clause_ids with polarity and channel** (opaque identifiers only — no clause text). They must not retain raw clause text, uploaded document text, model-written rationales, summaries, or substantive primary/critic deltas. Those fields are Confidential document substance and live only in retention-governed storage.

## Document retention and purge safety

Document retention is admin-configurable and enforced by a **purge worker**, not a static S3 lifecycle rule, because the window acts retroactively. The setting is either a bounded window (0 days–3 years, default 90) or the explicit **`forever` / indefinite preservation** sentinel (issue #34) — never purged, at any age. `forever` is a real sentinel value, not a large day-count: a bounded integer always eventually ages past its own window, so "never purge" has to be modeled as its own state, not as `MAX_RETENTION_WINDOW_DAYS` or larger. `forever` exists specifically for records tied to executed agreements or other matters a General Counsel wants answerable indefinitely rather than the 90-day default — see [docs/audit-queries.md](audit-queries.md) → "Substance retention boundary" for what "answerable" means at each tier. The mechanics live in [ARCHITECTURE.md](../ARCHITECTURE.md) (Storage) and the operator action in [RUNBOOK.md](../RUNBOOK.md) (Changing document retention). This section is the authoritative statement of **what the purge worker is and is not allowed to delete.**

A retroactive, admin-driven purge is powerful enough to destroy a document mid-pipeline or delete something that is being relied on. The purge worker is constrained by five invariants:

1. **Terminal reviews only.** The worker deletes documents belonging to reviews in a **terminal** state (`DONE`, `ERROR`, `MANUAL_REVIEW_REQUIRED`, `ERROR_MANUAL_REVIEW_REQUIRED`, `QUARANTINED`, `SUPERSEDED`). A review in `PENDING` or `RUNNING` is an **active execution** and is **excluded** from every sweep. A `0`-day window therefore never deletes the input of a review that is still being processed — even an immediate purge-all skips in-flight work. The worker resolves a document's review by the `{owner-sub}/{review-id}` key prefix and checks the `reviews` row's status before deleting; no status row, no delete.
2. **Snapshot-at-creation.** The retention window applied to a document is the window **in effect when the review was created** — a bounded day-count or `forever` — snapshotted onto the `reviews` row (`retention_window_at_creation`) at creation time. Later changes to the global setting apply going forward and to *terminal* reviews on the next sweep, but the value used for any given document is its own snapshot, so a review cannot have its governing window silently changed out from under it while it runs. A review snapshotted at `forever` is excluded from every sweep unconditionally, the same way an active (non-terminal) review is excluded by invariant 1 — just permanently rather than until it finishes.
3. **Legal hold overrides everything.** A document whose review (or whose corpus source) carries an active legal hold is **never** deleted by the worker, regardless of window, regardless of a `0`-day setting, regardless of the document's age. Hold is checked on every candidate object on every sweep and on the immediate retroactive sweep. See [Legal hold](#legal-hold) below.
4. **Documents, then matched substance fields.** When the worker deletes a document it also clears the Confidential substance fields (`verdict_summary`, `issue_rationale_text`) on the matching terminal `reviews` row, per the classification table above. The non-substantive audit-bearing fields remain.
5. **Dual-control or mandatory delay for retroactive reductions.** A retroactive reduction — lowering the retention window below its current value, where `forever` outranks every bounded window (so moving away from `forever` to any finite window is always a reduction, and moving to `forever` never is) — is a single-admin, immediately destructive action on unversioned S3 objects. Because audit records destruction rather than preventing it, and because the admin-UI XSS path (see [docs/threat-model.md](threat-model.md) → Malicious admin or compromised session) makes this action reachable by session compromise, retroactive reductions require one of the following before the immediate retroactive sweep is permitted to run: (a) **second admin confirmation** — a different admin confirms the change in the current session, with both actors' identities logged to `audit`; or (b) **72-hour pending delay** — the reduction enters a *pending* state, a GC alarm fires immediately notifying the General Counsel, and the sweep executes automatically at the end of the delay unless cancelled. Forward-looking changes (raising the window, or future-effective dates) are not retroactive reductions and continue to apply single-admin. This invariant is enforced server-side; the API rejects a retroactive-reduction sweep lacking a valid second-admin confirmation token or an expired delay. The operator procedure is in [RUNBOOK.md](../RUNBOOK.md) → Changing document retention.

The worker runs in two modes — an **immediate retroactive sweep** on save (the source of the destructive retroactive behavior, now gated by invariant 5) and a **scheduled sweep** going forward — and both modes enforce all five invariants identically. Every purge run writes an `audit` entry (objects considered, deleted, skipped-for-active, skipped-for-hold).

## Legal hold

**Admin-configurable retroactive deletion without a legal-hold override is an evidence-destruction risk.** A retention slider that lets an admin set `0` days and immediately purge everything must not be able to destroy a document that is subject to litigation hold, regulatory inquiry, or an internal investigation. Legal hold is the override that makes the retention slider safe, and it is a **prerequisite**: the retroactive purge capability must not be enabled in an environment until legal hold is in place.

### Hold flags

Two independent legal-hold flags exist:

- **Per-review hold.** A `legal_hold` boolean (plus `legal_hold_reason`, `legal_hold_set_by`, `legal_hold_set_at`) on the `reviews` row. While set, the review's `uploads`/`outputs` documents and its Confidential substance fields are exempt from every purge sweep, and the row's substance fields are **not** cleared.
- **Corpus hold.** A `legal_hold` flag on a corpus source document. While set, the corpus object is exempt from any corpus lifecycle action and cannot be re-ingested-away or deleted until released. The application flag is mirrored to storage through S3 Object Lock legal hold or a protected hold tag covered by bucket-policy denies. (The `corpus` bucket already uses governance-mode object lock per [ARCHITECTURE.md](../ARCHITECTURE.md) Storage; governance bypass is restricted to MFA break-glass with reason/ticket session tags and alarms, not routine deletion.)

### Override semantics

A hold **overrides retention unconditionally**. Specifically:

- An active hold beats any retention window, including `0` days.
- An active hold beats the immediate retroactive sweep on save.
- An active hold beats the scheduled sweep.
- An active hold prevents the matched-substance-field clear, so a held review keeps its `verdict_summary` and `issue_rationale_text`.

Retention can only ever delete what is **both** terminal **and** not under hold. There is no admin setting, and no single human action, that deletes a held document.

### Placing and releasing a hold

Placing or releasing a hold is itself a sensitive, audited action — it changes what evidence survives.

- **Placing** a hold (per-review or corpus) writes an `audit` entry recording actor, target, timestamp, and the required free-text reason. The hold takes effect immediately; the next sweep (and the on-save sweep) honors it.
- **Releasing** a hold writes a separate `audit` entry with actor, target, timestamp, and reason. A released document re-enters normal retention on the next sweep — it is **not** immediately deleted on release; it simply becomes eligible again under its snapshotted window.
- Holds are **append-only in spirit**: the `audit` table is deny-`UpdateItem`/deny-`DeleteItem` for all app roles (see [ARCHITECTURE.md](../ARCHITECTURE.md) Audit posture), so the history of who held and released what cannot be rewritten. Every storage-level hold/tag change and every governance-bypass attempt is audited and alarmed.

The operator procedure — who may place a hold, how to scope it to a counterparty or date range, and how to confirm a sweep honored it — lives in [RUNBOOK.md](../RUNBOOK.md) (legal-hold procedure). Threats against the hold mechanism (e.g. an admin attempting destruction via the retention slider) are enumerated in [docs/threat-model.md](threat-model.md).

## Step Functions execution-history classification

Step Functions records the input and output of every state in execution history — retained by the service, visible in the AWS console, and subject to the same CMK encryption the state machine is configured with. This store must be treated as an observability surface, not a confidential data store.

**Pointer-only payload rule (authoritative statement).** State payloads carry **non-substantive content only**:

| Allowed in SFN payload | Prohibited in SFN payload |
|------------------------|---------------------------|
| `review_id`, `owner_sub` | Document text (extracted or raw) |
| S3 bucket + object key references | Prompt bodies |
| `input_doc_hash`, `output_doc_hash` | Model output (JSON or prose) |
| `playbook_hash`, `prompt_hash`, `standard_form_hash`, `model_policy_hash` | Retrieved precedent text or clause text |
| `corpus_snapshot_version`, `physical_store_id` | Model rationales, summaries, critic deltas |
| `status`, `confidence_state`, `decision` | `verdict_summary`, `issue_rationale_text` |
| `tokens_in`, `tokens_out`, `cost_usd` | Any field classified as Confidential in the field dictionary above |
| `execution_arn`, step-level status and timestamps | |

Document text and all derived substantive content travel via the encrypted `uploads`/`outputs` S3 buckets, not inline between Step Functions states. Each pipeline step reads from and writes to those buckets; the Step Functions payload carries only the references needed to locate the data.

**CMK.** The state machine is configured with a customer-managed KMS key (issue #19, `AppStack.stateMachineKey`). Execution-history records are encrypted under this key. The key policy is consistent with the per-data-class posture: only the pipeline task role receives the grants needed to use the key.

**Execution-history substance scan (pending #59).** A pipeline integration test asserting that no `GetExecutionHistory` event contains document text, prompt text, or model output is a #59 acceptance criterion and will gate every subsequent pipeline change in CI once the real state machine is delivered. Until then the pointer-only rule is enforced by convention and code review.

**Why this is not in the `reviews` field dictionary.** Step Functions execution-history records are service-managed state, not application-managed rows. They are not classified per the `reviews` field dictionary above; instead the pointer-only rule above is the governing constraint. Non-substantive content may appear in execution history indefinitely; it carries no document substance, so no retention window applies.

**Residency rule.** All subprocessor data flows must remain within `us-east-1` or be explicitly approved by the General Counsel. Data residency is a non-negotiable constraint; adding a subprocessor that routes data outside `us-east-1` without GC approval is a policy violation.

**AV implementation note.** The AV approach is in-account ClamAV to ensure uploaded documents — which may contain confidential counterparty agreement text — never leave the AWS account boundary during scanning. Cloud-based AV services that transmit sample bytes or hashes to a vendor cloud would silently break the `us-east-1` residency and confidentiality guarantees for every upload. The in-account approach eliminates this risk at the cost of managing ClamAV definition updates in-account. See [docs/threat-model.md](threat-model.md) → Hostile file uploads for the full scanning gauntlet.

## Summary of guarantees

- Every uploaded or generated document is treated as **Confidential** by default; the **Restricted** tier's controls (named-access lists, key separation, mandatory hold eligibility) are scaffolded in v1 so a regulated-content document drops in without re-architecting.
- "Metadata retained indefinitely" means **non-substantive** metadata only. Model-written summaries and rationales are Confidential and expire with their document; only identifiers, hashes, the decision, and cost/version facts persist indefinitely.
- The purge worker deletes **only terminal reviews**, on each review's **snapshotted** window, **never** an active execution, and **never** a held document — including under a `0`-day retroactive purge.
- **Legal hold overrides retention unconditionally** and is a prerequisite for enabling retroactive purge. Placing and releasing holds is audited on an append-only, immutable audit table.
- **Retroactive reductions require dual-control or a 72-hour delay with GC alarm** (purge invariant 5). A single-admin retroactive reduction is rejected server-side; a second admin must confirm or the sweep is held pending for 72 hours with the General Counsel notified by alarm.
- **Derived corpus artifacts** (S3 Vectors embeddings, staging indexes, clause-text store) are classified Confidential, encrypted under the corpus CMK, and lifecycle-bound to corpus snapshot manifests. Corpus legal hold extends to all derived artifacts.
- **Subprocessors** are enumerated in the Third parties / subprocessors section above. All data flows remain within `us-east-1` unless explicitly GC-approved. AV scanning is in-account (ClamAV in Lambda) with no external sample transmission.
